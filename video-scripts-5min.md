# Portfolio video scripts — 5 minute versions

Four 5-minute scripts for the hero-video slots on `index.html`, `lycia.html`, `science37.html`, and `remedly.html`. Longer companions to the 2-minute scripts in `video-scripts.md` — pick whichever length fits the slot.

Pacing target: **150–160 words per minute** (conversational, not rushed). Each script is ~770–800 words. Read once for time before recording — natural pauses and screen pointing usually push it slightly past 5 minutes, which is fine.

All four close with `tuncellih@gmail.com`.

## 2. Lycia AI (`lycia.html`) — Director of Product portfolio

**Length:** ~775 words · ~5:00

> Hi, I'm Harun. This is the Lycia AI portfolio.
>
> Lycia is an autonomous AI agent for hospitality, deployed in hotels, airports, malls, restaurants, and offices. I'm the Director of Product. I shipped this end-to-end with our team, and I lead the strategy for what comes next.
>
> This page is structured as a six-stop narrative — how we started, the product we built, the pivot that reshaped the business model, the outcomes from the field, and where we're taking it next. I'll walk you through each stop.
>
> First — how we started. The page opens with the hotel AI market analysis I wrote — a forty-page document covering the demand drivers, the incumbent approaches, and the structural gap we set out to fill. Sitting next to it is a designed hospitality research piece — a real customer journey for a San Diego boutique hotel. That pairing — broad market plus one deep customer — is how we evaluated every product decision in the first eighteen months.
>
> Second — the product we created. Two pieces. The live product at lyciaai.com, which is the public-facing surface for the autonomous agent. And the Beau Hotel walkthrough, which is the full guest journey — pre-arrival, check-in, in-stay requests, post-stay — running on a real customer property. That walkthrough is the artifact I'd hand a hiring manager who wants to know what the product actually does, end to end.
>
> Third — and this is the section I'd ask a reviewer to spend the most time in — the pivot. We started as a guest-facing concierge agent. Twelve months in, looking at the data, we realized the bigger opportunity was a multi-sided AI commerce network. The thesis is simple. Every hotel guest is also a restaurant guest, a bar guest, a venue guest, and an activities guest. If the agent can transact across all five verticals, every new participant on the supply side makes the network more valuable for everyone already on it. The page walks each vertical — hotels, bars and lounges, restaurants, music venues, activities — with the partnership and revenue-engine pitch deck for each. Five sales artifacts I built alongside the product strategy.
>
> Fourth — outcomes. Three composite customer deployments with ninety-day metrics — bookings handled, response-time deltas, dollar-level revenue lift, the percentage of guest messages the agent could close without staff escalation. The composites are anonymized but the numbers are real. And the section I'm proudest of from a product-judgment perspective: a first-person walkthrough of an AI-first inbox decision I shipped. What I considered. What I chose. The thirty-day metrics that came back. And one sequencing mistake I'd undo if I could rewind — we shipped the auto-translation feature before we'd built the override path, and a small percentage of replies went out half-translated for the first week. That's the kind of self-critique I think is missing from most portfolios.
>
> Fifth — what comes next. The expansion plan across all five verticals, plus the AI-native PMS thesis. The thesis is that the long tail of independent hotels — the thousands of properties that can't justify a real PMS license — is the biggest unaddressed opportunity in hospitality software. Our overlay model doesn't reach them today. A PMS purpose-built for that segment, bundled at nineteen-ninety to twenty-four-ninety per room per month, undercuts the unbundled twenty-five to thirty-five dollar stack independent hotels currently cobble together.
>
> Which brings us to the concept demo at the bottom of the page. Six surfaces of an AI-first PMS — home dashboard, AI-first inbox, automations, integrations, rate management, and employees. About twenty-five thousand lines of working front-end code with localStorage persistence. It's a concept, not a shipping product. But it's real working software — you can click into any guest, send a message, watch the agent draft a reply, and see the end-to-end flow. If you've ever wanted to know what an AI-native PMS would actually feel like to operate, this is the closest you'll get without building one yourself.
>
> The decks section bundles all the partnership and revenue-engine pitches. Sixteen PDFs in total. The research section holds the strategy thesis, the market analysis, and the designed customer journey.
>
> If you're in product or design and want to talk hospitality AI — or if you're hiring for Senior PM or Director of Product roles — I'd love to connect.

## 3. Science 37 (`science37.html`) — Sr. Product Designer, Visit Operations Platform

**Length:** ~790 words · ~5:00

> Hi, I'm Harun. This is the Visit Operations Platform portfolio from Science 37.
>
> Visit Operations Platform is the scheduling system that powers Science 37's decentralized clinical trials. I was Senior Product Designer. I led the end-to-end design — discovery research, concept validation, the shipped UI, and the product architecture behind it.
>
> Before I walk the page, one reframing. This portfolio is not pitched as a scheduling feature set. It's pitched as a metro-focus operating-model substrate. Sci 37 collapsed in part because of footprint mismatch — too many states, too few clinicians per metro, too much windshield time per visit. The scheduling efficiency story is real but modest. The bigger story is what the prototype implies — if Sci 37 had concentrated its footprint and built around an engine like this one, the revenue uplift could be in the fifty to one-hundred-thirty-five-million-dollar range. That's the framing the page leads with.
>
> The page is structured as a six-stop narrative. Let me walk each stop.
>
> First — how we started. Pre-build problem validation. Before any pixels, I shadowed schedulers, mapped the manual workflow, and ran concept-validation interviews to make sure the design we were about to build actually answered what they asked for. The page links the discovery PDF and the concept-validation deck.
>
> Second — the product we created. This is where I'd want a reviewer to spend the most time. Six features — and I want to call out three.
>
> Optimize Day is a 2-opt drive-time optimizer. It treats a nurse's day as a Travelling Salesman instance anchored at her home address. Nearest-neighbor seed, 2-opt local search, before-and-after diff side by side. One click to accept the shorter route. That feature alone moved hours of back-office work each week into one or two button presses.
>
> Smart Match is the second. One click pairs the right RN, the right CRC, and the right investigator with the right visit — filtered by license, study training, and drive distance from each clinician's home address. Schedulers used to spend the first hour of every morning making those pairings by hand on a whiteboard.
>
> The command palette — Command-K — is the third. Schedulers book without leaving the keyboard. Type a participant name, pick a date, the system surfaces the eligible providers and the visit type, and the booking is in. For a high-velocity operations team, removing every modal-and-dropdown click compounds.
>
> Three more features round out the section. A unified workspace that puts the entire scheduling team in one tool instead of three. A per-role visit timeline that shows every clinician's actual minutes on every visit — so when a scheduler sees a conflict, the conflict is the real conflict, not a calendar artifact. And a time-off auto-resolution flow that took eighty-nine pending requests and closed eighty-seven of them with no scheduler legwork — the system identified the coverage, surfaced the swap, and the scheduler only saw the two it couldn't resolve.
>
> Third — the shipped UI gallery. Eleven screenshots of the actual product, regenerated from the live app. And the case study PDF — the full deliverable that walks every surface with the design rationale behind each decision. If you want the deepest read, that's the document.
>
> Fourth — the decision that shaped everything. The roster-first architecture. We made a structural call early on that the data model would treat the clinician roster as the primary entity, with everything else — visits, studies, participants — joining off it. That choice unlocked the optimizer, unlocked Smart Match, and unlocked the per-provider day view. The page explains the call and includes the architecture document.
>
> Fifth — outcomes, in numbers. Four metrics. 1:42 CRC against an industry norm of 1:15 to 1:30 — meaning each clinical research coordinator at Sci 37 was supporting almost three times the industry standard. 1:12 RN — that's at the strict end of the hospice nursing range of 1:12 to 1:14. 1:71 INV — investigator coverage with no real industry comparator because nobody else operates at that span. And the engine-first revenue uplift estimate: fifty to one-hundred-thirty-five million dollars per year at a six-metro footprint.
>
> Sixth — the strategic case the prototype builds. Two decks. The optimizer engine proposal — what it would take to rebuild Sci 37 around this engine. And the DCT market analysis — sizing the decentralized trials market and the scheduling-tool ROI within it. The two together are the executive read for anyone who wants to understand how a designer's prototype becomes a company-level operating thesis.
>
> Email me at tuncellih@gmail.com.

## 4. Remedly (`remedly.html`) — UI/UX Designer · EHR rebuild

**Length:** ~785 words · ~5:00

> Hi, I'm Harun. This is the Remedly portfolio.
>
> Remedly was a healthcare startup building an electronic health record system. I joined as a UI/UX Designer and was the only designer in the company at the time. The product I inherited had two-decade-old design — interface patterns from a different era of the web. The mandate was to rebuild it without breaking the practices already running on it.
>
> This page is structured as a six-stop narrative. Let me walk each stop.
>
> First — the introduction sets the context. The EHR market in the late twenty-tens was one of the most documented usability failures in software. Every major healthcare-IT research piece — Sinsky, RAND, Pew, KLAS — was converging on the same finding. EHRs were the leading driver of physician burnout. We had to do better.
>
> Second — pre-product research. This is the section I'd point any reviewer to first, because it's the work most designers skip. I ran the customer-experience signals audit before drawing a single screen. Four numbers came back. Seventy-eight percent of users reported workflow friction during clinical encounters. Sixty-nine percent said they spent more time on documentation than patient care. Seventy-five percent rated usability at the bottom of competing systems. Seventy-two percent said they would switch if a better option existed. The pre-product research PDF on this page walks all four signals plus the competitor benchmark — head-to-head against Athena, EPIC, and the smaller niche systems — across medical content, workflow speed, features, and usability and UI. From that benchmark came the action plan that scoped what we'd actually build.
>
> Third — the product we created. Three products, in order of ship.
>
> The web EHR rebuild — the practitioner workspace. Notes, prescriptions, billing, scheduling. I redesigned every primary surface and rebuilt the design system underneath. The page shows the before and after where the contrast is most visible.
>
> The patient mobile app — the second card. Gamified intake forms so patients actually fill them out before they walk in, plus in-app communication with the practice. That mobile app turned a thirty-percent intake completion rate into something north of ninety percent within ninety days at the pilot practices.
>
> And the third — the one I'm proudest of for the timing — a 2019 voice-AI concept for patient-visit capture. The doctor talks, the system transcribes, structures, and files. We built the concept in 2019, five years before the current wave of medical voice-AI products like Suki, Nuance DAX, and Abridge entered the market.
>
> Fourth — the decision that shaped everything. Design for the next ten years, not the next quarter. Every redesign cycle in EHR-land at the time was patching the old patterns. We made the call to rebuild from the ground up — pay the short-term cost of retraining existing users, earn the long-term benefit of an interface that wouldn't need to be rebuilt again in three years. The page walks the reasoning.
>
> Fifth — post-product result research. After the redesign shipped, we measured. Charting time down forty-three percent. Documentation cycle time down sixty to seventy percent. Clicks-per-encounter down sixty percent. And over a hundred pages of qualitative feedback from practices that had been on the system for at least six months. Those numbers — especially the documentation time reduction — are the kind of outcome you can take into a hospital procurement conversation and close on.
>
> Remedly was where I learned that healthcare design isn't about pixel polish. It's about removing minutes from a clinician's day so they can give those minutes back to a patient. Operational reality first, visual craft second — that's the through-line from this work to everything I've done since.
>
> Email me at tuncellih@gmail.com.

## Recording notes

- **Pace:** read at the pace you'd talk to a hiring manager — not slow, not rushed.
- **Numbers:** spell out percentages and counts naturally ("forty-three percent," "twenty-five thousand"). Already written that way above.
- **B-roll cues** *(optional)*: when you mention a specific section, deck, or feature, the screen can cut to that artifact. Most natural cuts — the case study PDF cover when you say "the case study walks every surface," the Optimize Day screenshot when you mention the 2-opt optimizer, the AI-first inbox surface when you mention "twenty-five thousand lines of working code."
- **Outro:** all four end with the same email — keeps the call to action consistent across the videos.
- **Re-recording:** if a take runs over 5:30, trim the middle section (typically the feature deep-dive), not the intro or the outro. The intro positions you and the outro is the call to action — both are load-bearing.
